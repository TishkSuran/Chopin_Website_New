BIOGRAPHY

Frédéric Chopin

A luminary of the Romantic era whose poetic genius at the piano transformed the musical landscape of the 19th century and continues to captivate audiences today.

The Life of a Musical Genius

Frédéric François Chopin (1 March 1810 – 17 October 1849) was a Polish composer and virtuoso pianist of the Romantic period who wrote primarily for solo piano. He has maintained worldwide renown as a leading composer of his era whose "poetic genius was based on a professional technique that was without equal in his generation".

The biographical information on this page is summarised from Wikipedia's Frédéric Chopin article.

Timeline of Chopin's Life

1810

Early Years

Frédéric François Chopin was born on March 1, 1810 in Żelazowa Wola, near Warsaw, in what was then the Duchy of Warsaw. His father, Nicolas Chopin, was a Frenchman from Lorraine who had emigrated to Poland, while his mother, Justyna Krzyżanowska, was Polish. Shortly after his birth, the family moved to Warsaw, where his father became a teacher of French at the Warsaw Lyceum.

Even as a child, Chopin displayed remarkable musical talent. By the age of seven, he was already giving public concerts and had composed his first polonaises.

1816-1830

Musical Education

From 1816 to 1821, Chopin received piano lessons from the Czech musician Wojciech Żywny. His extraordinary talent was quickly recognized, and between 1823 and 1826, he studied at the Warsaw Lyceum.

In 1826, Chopin began a three-year course under Józef Elsner at the Warsaw Conservatory, studying music theory and composition. During this time, he continued to compose and perform in concerts and salons around Warsaw.

The success of his performances and compositions opened doors to Western Europe. In 1829, he made his debut in Vienna where he received favorable reviews, premiering his "Variations on Là ci darem la mano" for piano and orchestra.

1830-1831

Departure from Poland

On November 2, 1830, Chopin left Warsaw for Vienna, intending to establish his career in Western Europe. Shortly after his departure, the November 1830 Uprising broke out in Poland. When he learned that the rebellion against Russian rule had been crushed, he expressed deep anguish for his homeland.

Unable to return to a Poland under oppressive Russian control, Chopin became one of many expatriates of the Polish Great Emigration. He would never return to his homeland.

1831-1838

Life in Paris

Chopin arrived in Paris on October 5, 1831. In the French capital, he encountered many distinguished figures of the artistic world, including Hector Berlioz, Franz Liszt, Ferdinand Hiller, Heinrich Heine, and Eugène Delacroix.

By late 1832, Chopin had established himself among the Parisian musical elite and had earned the respect of his peers. He began earning a comfortable income from publishing his works and teaching piano to affluent students from across Europe.

Rather than public concerts, Chopin preferred playing in salons or at his own Paris apartment for small groups of friends. Throughout his career, he gave remarkably few public performances—only about thirty in his lifetime.

In 1836, Chopin met the French author George Sand (born Amantine Aurore Lucile Dupin) at a party hosted by Marie d'Agoult. Despite his initial impression that she was unattractive, by July 1838, they had become lovers.

1838-1847

Relationship with George Sand

Chopin and Sand, along with her two children, spent the winter of 1838-39 on Majorca, hoping to improve Chopin's deteriorating health. The trip proved difficult due to the island's inhospitable weather and the locals' disapproval of their unmarried status.

Despite the challenges, this period was musically productive for Chopin. He completed his set of 24 Preludes, Op. 28, as well as several other compositions.

From 1839 to 1846, Chopin and Sand spent most summers at her estate in Nohant, France. During these peaceful summers, Chopin composed many significant works, including his Polonaise in A-flat major, Op. 53.

Their relationship ended in 1847 after growing tensions, particularly involving Sand's children and political differences.

1842-1849

Final Years

By 1842, Chopin showed serious signs of illness. His health continued to deteriorate, and his compositional output declined year by year. Speculation about his illness has included tuberculosis, cystic fibrosis, and other conditions.

In February 1848, with the cellist Auguste Franchomme, Chopin gave his last Paris concert. In April, during the Revolution in Paris, he left for London at the suggestion of his Scottish pupil Jane Stirling.

In London and Scotland, Chopin performed at several concerts and taught piano lessons, but his health was rapidly failing. He returned to Paris in November 1848, weighing less than 45 kg (99 lb).

His condition worsened throughout 1849. In June, his sister Ludwika came to Paris to care for him. On October 17, 1849, surrounded by close friends, Chopin died in Paris at the age of 39. His funeral at the Church of the Madeleine featured Mozart's Requiem and drew over 3,000 people.

"Bach is an astronomer, discovering the most marvelous stars. Beethoven challenges the universe. I only try to express the soul and the heart of man."
— Frédéric Chopin

Historical Impact

Chopin's music, his status as one of music's earliest celebrities, his association with Polish nationalism, his high-profile relationship with George Sand, and his early death have made him a leading symbol of the Romantic era.

His compositions continue to be central to the piano repertoire and are performed by pianists worldwide. The International Chopin Piano Competition, established in 1927, is one of the oldest and most prestigious music competitions in the world.

Beyond his musical innovations, Chopin's influence extends to literature, film, and popular culture. His life and music have inspired countless artists across many disciplines, cementing his place as one of history's most beloved and influential composers.
